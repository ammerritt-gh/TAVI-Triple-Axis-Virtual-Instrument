## Abstract 

The primary spectrometer of ILL’s thermal three-axis spectrometer IN8 has undergone a major rebuild during the last few years. We here summarise the characteristics of the new instrument and highlight its enhanced performance in terms of flexibility and luminosity. Flux measurements indicate an increase of monochromatic flux at the sample position of x 3– x 5 depending on monochromator and wavelength as compared to the previous instrument IN8B. ## 1. Introduction 

Three-axis spectroscopy (TAS) is a well-known and widely used technique for the study of excitations in condensed matter. An introduction to the TAS technique can be found in many textbooks and a recent review on the principles as well as technical aspects is given in Ref. [1]. TAS is the technique of choice whenever it is desirable to perform selected scans to explore specific regions in momentum Q and energy E space. The relevant quantities for a TAS instrument are luminosity, resolution widths ðDQ; DEÞ and the signal-to-noise ratio. As inelastic neutron scattering is intensity limited, ‘‘flexibility’’ is an important issue; it means being able to trade in the most practical way resolution against luminosity. 

The recent upgrade of the thermal three-axis spectrometer IN8 aimed to optimise the primary spectrometer for those needs. The ILL has undertaken the project in collaboration with its Spanish scientific partners. The previous instrument had been decommissioned at the end of 1999. ## 2. Primary spectrometer 

IN8 is optimised for a horizontally focusing beam geometry, which allows increasing the monochromatic flux at the sample at the expense of momentum resolution but not energy resolution [2]. Monte Carlo simulations [3] using the Restrax package have been performed prior to the upgrade to optimise the instrumental parameters and configuration. Technical details of the primary spectrometer are summarised in Ref. [4]: An adjustable horizontal slit device made of (fast) neutron shielding materials is introduced as a horizontal virtual source (HVS) at a fixed distance d ¼ 2284 mm before the monochromator. ## 4. Scientific impact 

IN8 is optimised for inelastic measurements with an energy transfer in the range of a few meV to about 100meV and is used to investigate magnetic excitations, lattice vibrations and excitations in liquids. Its high incident flux permits to investigate sample of small volume and weak inelastic response. 

IN8 provides the highest monochromatic flux of all thermal three-axis spectrometers. In comparison, the thermal TAS instruments at ILL appear to be quite complementary: IN8 and IN20 have similar unpolarised flux when working with the Si111 monochromator. But since IN20 is optimised for polarised neutron experiments, IN8 offers a wider range of flexibility for optimising the flux and resolution conditions. IN22 (CRG) is installed on a thermal guide. At sub-thermal energies and when a limited beam-divergence is required, it provides comparable flux to IN8. Nevertheless IN8 will outperform IN22 under full focusing conditions and at elevated energies. In a European context, IN8 might be compared to the thermal instruments 1T1 at LLB as well as PUMA at FRMII. No data exist for the later, but the beam geometry is very similar to IN8’s. Performance will hence compare and only depend on source performance. Experiments on similar samples on 1T1 and IN8 provide indications of an effective gain in counting rates of x 3– x 5 over 1T1.

## 5. Outlook 

The monochromatic flux at the sample position is shown in Fig. 4. The measurements were performed using ILL’s calibrated monitor. All measured values were corrected for higher order contamination at each ki using the calibrated (Pyrex) absorber technique [6]. Additional measurements at ki ¼ 2:662 and 4:1 A[1] were performed with a PG filter and confirm those results. The monochromatic flux increased by a factor x 3– x 5 depending on monochromator and wavelength with respect to the previous instrument. These values agree well with those predicted by Monte Carlo simulations [3] taking the x 2:2 increase of white flux into account. It should be pointed out that the energy resolution is improved for all three monochromator faces: The PG002 crystals are better aligned and the effective mosaic is therefore smaller than before, the d-spacing of Cu200 is smaller than that of the previously used Cu111 and the Si111 face consists in bent perfect crystals. 

When optimising the experimental conditions, the signalto-noise ratio obtained on IN8 compares well with respect to other thermal TAS instruments. Most often the background is given by the sample, hence the signal-tonoise ratio is fixed. Also tests with well-shaped lowbackground samples (like cupper-germanate) confirm this statement. 

Currently IN8 is mostly operated with large doublefocusing PG002, Cu111 and Si111 analysers with a wide scattered beam acceptance angle mirroring the focusing conditions of the primary spectromenter. Currently IMPS [7], an imaging multiplexed spectrometer, is being developed. This flexible, imaging multianalyser–detector system will allow to measure several points in ðQ; EÞ space simultaneously. Furthermore, first experiments were performed with the multiplexed analyser and detector (MAD) arrangement [8] and IN8 will accommodate the TAS flatcone option recently commissioned [9] as a mapping facility covering a wide Q-range at constant energy transfer.
