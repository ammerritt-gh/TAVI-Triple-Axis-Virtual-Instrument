## 1. Introduction 

## 2. Overview of PANDA 

Neutron scattering is being extensively used during the past half a century, shedding light across a plethora of condensed matter physics problems ranging from crystallography to excitations, quantum magnetism, superconductivity, topological and other new forms of matter. The instrument PANDA at the neutron source FRM II is one of the world-leading triple-axis spectrometers, serving an international community for the past 12 years [1]. It is currently operated with a double-focusing pyrolitic graphite (PG) monochromator, which provides a beam size of 34 mm in diameter at the sample position. 

Current trends shift towards more complex materials, often with smaller sizes. Further, the sample environment conditions are more stringent, with high fields, low temperatures and high pressures often used in combination. Signal-to-noise ratios have to be reduced, especially for the case of pressure cells, where the surrounding material is substantially thick and placed in the direct vicinity of the sample. To improve the performance of the PANDA instrument in such cases, the neutron beam needs to be focused such that only the sample itself is illuminated, excluding the surrounding material. 

The triple-axis spectrometer PANDA [1] is located at a distance of 7.8 m from the cold neutron source. The extracted neutrons are transported to the instrument through a neutron channel and enter the monochromator drum through a horizontal virtual source. The monochromator selects a specific energy via Bragg reflection, the beam scatters at the sample and is finally energyanalysed and detected. The focusing guide shall be installed between the monochromator and sample, therefore its length is limited to be within 2.1 m. The available space is further restricted by the size of the sample environment and components near the monochromator, giving a maximum possible length of 1.48 m for the focusing guide, as shown in Fig. 1. 

In order to simulate the performance of different focusing geometries, we used the McStas v2.2 Monte-Carlo package [2,3]. Our virtual instrument model includes all components from the source to the sample, representing an accurate description of PANDA. Previous experience has shown that such simulations agree very well with the experimental results [4,5].

## 3. Focusing devices and their properties 

## 3.1. Monochromator 

The standard focusing device of a triple axis spectrometer is a focusing monochromator. This enables one to control the spotsize, intensity and energy resolution at once [6,7]. Optimum focusing conditions depend on whether the beam reaching the monochromator comes from a point source at distance L or is parallel ( _L_ →∞). More complex cases in between can be met, where the most general analytic results should be used [8]. Spatial dimensions of the beam play a role, especially in the case of a virtual slit, where ideally it should be small ( ≈ 10 mm) for good monochromatic focusing. The PANDA monochromator focuses in both vertical and horizontal directions. It consists of an 11 × 11 array of pyrolytic graphite single crystals which can be curved [1]. The beam is vertically extended and parallel, whilst horizontally it is point-source-like and divergent due to the virtual source which has a rectangular shape. The minimum beam size achievable with PANDA's double focusing monochromator is 34 mm in diameter and limited by the size of the individual single crystal pieces (20 × 20 mm[2] ) and their mosaic spread (20 inch). This is shown in Fig. 2 a. We wish to improve the focusing to a smaller beam size, by the use of focusing neutron guides. 

## 3.2. Focusing guides 

Neutron supermirror guides have been first implemented at the spallation source SINQ in 2004 and are now extensively used at neutron scattering facilities in order to transport neutrons and enhance outcoming flux [9,10]. Special tapering shapes (linear, elliptical, parabolic, etc.) can be used in order to focus the beam further [11]. For triple-axis spectrometers, this is typically done with the so-called virtual source concept, which takes place before the monochromator [4,12,13]. A virtual source also ensures that strict monochromatic conditions are achieved. It is particularly useful for the case of long-guide instruments, where the real neutron source is far away from the sample. A correctly implemented virtual source ensures that the instrument stays in symmetric Rowland focusing geometry. 

PANDA shall use such a focusing guide in a different way. To reduce the beam size further i.e., the neutron focusing guide is placed between the monochromator and sample. The number of reflections is reduced to 1 or 2, thus keeping the phase space compact [14,15]. 

Fig. 2. Spatial distribution of intensity at the sample position (vertical and hor1 izontal axes) for the setups of Table 1 for _ki_ = 1.5 Å[−] : (a) focusing by the monochromator, (b) elliptic focusing assuming a 1300 mm long guide, (c) parabolic focusing for a 1480 mm guide and (d) elliptic focusing for a 1480 mm guide. All but the first produce small spot sizes with the last one having the best properties in terms of spot size, sharpness and intensity. 

The most important advantage of parabolic and elliptic focusing guides is that both give very sharp spotsizes and therefore come along with excellent signal to noise ratios. They help illuminate only the sample area, such as required in complex sample environment conditions (pressure cells, etc.). They differ in their concept of imaging. In the case of a parabolic shape, a parallel beam can be converged on a single point. In contrast, a point-to-point imaging is performed by an elliptic guide. In order to optimize, it is critical to check for both cases with McStas Monte Carlo simulations. 

## 4. Results and discussion 

## 4.1. Optimizing neutron focusing guide parameters 

The maximum length which fits on PANDA between the monochromator and the sample environment is 1480 mm. To keep inhomogeneities in the divergence distribution to a minimum, the guide should be as long as possible [14,15]. For optimal focusing, the sample position must coincide with the focal point of the parabola/ellipse. 

In Fig. 1, we show how an ellipse is transformed under the symmetry of the monochromator. For parabolic guides, F1 is at infinity. Once the position of the guide is fixed, its entrance size can be optimized. According to Liouville's theorem, the product of beam size and divergence remains constant, assuming no beam losses. For small sample sizes and an upper limit on accepted divergences (typical maxima ±2°), Liouville's theorem estimates an entrance opening of ≈40 × 40 mm[2] for PANDA. Above that opening, the divergence of the transmitted neutrons will exceed ±2°, which is too large. 

The entrance opening was further numerically optimized with McStas for the parabolic and the elliptic cases. The result is that in both cases the optimum opening is found to be approximately 40 × 60 mm[2] by taking into account the same divergence constraints for width and height as for the analytical result above. This produces the sharpest spot size, and highest intensity as measured with a 3 × 3 mm[2] monitor. The result is in close agreement with the analytic estimation obtained by Liouville's theorem. We note that by further increasing the guide opening, no intensity is gained. It is saturated due to the beam surpassing the critical angle of the supermirror at the focusing guide.

## 4.2. Comparing parabolic and elliptical designs 

We implement the optimized elliptic and parabolic focusing guides in McStas, using a flat monochromator, and compare their performance. For benchmarking, pure focusing by the monochromator (without a guide) is also calculated. Apart from the maximum allowed length of 1480 mm, we also show a shorter version of 1300 mm, to allow for enlarged sample environments. All cases significantly reduce the spot size at the sample position and provide sharp edges. Starting from a 34 mm diameter spot size (focusing by the monochromator) we obtain a beam size of a few mm, as shown in Table 2 for both vertical and horizontal directions. In the best case (last row) the spot size is reduced by a factor of E25, providing an enormous advantage for the most stringent sample environment cases in terms of signal to noise ratio, particularly for setups with pressure cells. Further, the intensity over a 3 × 3 mm[2] area reveals that the elliptic guide transports twice as many neutrons to the sample as compared to the monochromator or parabolic focusing. The shorter ellipse (1300 mm) is outperformed by all other cases (see Table 2) and is therefore disregarded.

Let us now look at the divergence profiles, summarized in Fig. 3 (a) and (b) for the vertical and horizontal directions respectively. Focusing vertically with the monochromator produces a broad pattern with 11 peaks (one per monochromator column). The pattern changes significantly for elliptic or parabolic focusing. The advantage of focusing guides is that the patterns have a narrower distribution. The elliptic setup has also higher intensity, in particular the intensity at 0° divergence is doubled as compared to the focusing with the monochromator. This makes focusing with guides particularly attractive. Horizontally, the situation is somewhat alike, in the sense that a particular structure is replaced by another structure. Overall, the divergence is mostly kept within the desired 2° limit, however, with increased intensity. The elliptic guide with 1480 mm length outperforms all other designs and is therefore chosen for PANDA. The inhomogeneous divergence distribution is caused by the short length of the focusing guide [14]. The effects of focusing on neutron diffraction have been discussed in detail by Goncharenko et al. many years ago [16]. Of course the experimentalist has to take these effects properly into account during the planning phase of the experiment and the analysis of the data.

## 4.3. Energy dependence 

So far, we studied elliptic and parabolic focusing guides with a −1 fixed wave vector _ki_ = 1.5 Å ( _λi_ = 4.2 Å, _Ei_ = 4.7 meV) of the incident neutrons. We now focus on the effects of changing the incident energy, a situation typically encountered during a neutron scattering experiment at a triple-axis spectrometer. Possible effects of energy variation on the focusing properties of neutron guides are discussed in [17]. The following virtual experiment is performed in McStas, in order to visualize the effects: A PSD monitor is scanned along the beam direction, near the sample position. The resulting spot size (FWHM) is summarized in Fig. 4 for PANDA's range (1.5 Å[�][1] ≤ _ki_ ≤ 3 Å−1). Sharpest spot size is the minimum of each curve, with 0 being the nominal sample position as optimized for _ki_ = 1.5 Å. We notice that horizontally (Fig. 4a) the minima of all curves almost coincide near 0 mm, with very small variations along x. Vertically (Fig. 4b) minima can differ by as much as 10 mm. What is crucial is to notice that in fact the spot size for all curves is optimized at the dashed line of Fig. 4. This is due to the flatness of the peaks around the minima. In other words, there is no noticeable shift of the focal point for the range of parameters used at PANDA. What changes, however, is the size of the spot: it unavoidably increases with increasing ki, for both directions. 

In case the above mentioned wavelength variations were significant, one could have counteracted accordingly. There are at least three ways of getting the sharpest spot size possible: (A) shifting the sample position, (B) translating the guide forward or backward, along the optical axis (this would be a first-order 

approximation of A above) or, (C) by using a constant ki mode and vary kf. 

## 5. Conclusions 

We have shown that focusing with the monochromator on PANDA gives a spot size of 34 mm in diameter, mainly due to the size of the individual crystal pieces and mosaic spread. Based on the beam characteristics (size and divergence), one may need elliptic or parabolic beam focusing, to further decrease the spot size. For the PANDA triple-axis spectrometer we opted for an elliptically shaped guide with coating m¼ 6. This makes slits obsolete and drastically improves the signal-to-noise ratio. The guide's length, 1480 mm, is enough to provide optimum focusing conditions while keeping a 120 mm distance from the sample, allowing for sample environments such as cryostats and pressure cells. 

The parameters given in Table 1 yield a homogeneous beam with sharp boundaries. As compared to the double-focusing monochromator, we are able to reduce the beam size of 34 mm in diameter down to 8 × 6 mm[2] (a factor of 25). One of the goals was to maintain the divergence within the range of the monochromator focusing (2°). Hence the intensity is correspondingly increased by a factor of 2. Further, the new design is expected to reduce the noise level. This is mainly due to the sharp beam edges without slits, since slits provide a diffuse neutron distribution around the edges. Ideally one should use a more compact monochromator, illuminating only the guide entrance, in order to obtain an even better signal-to-noise ratio. Overall, PANDA is expected to significantly improve, for high pressure experiments, as a result of the drastic spot size reduction. This shall enable users to push the boundaries for such complex measurements further.
